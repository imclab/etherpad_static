2.a) How would you characterize the pre-1911 relationship between China and Tibet? Kerry Hall

                                   
The pre-1911 relationship between China and Tibet was largely one of indirect rule, as law professor Michael C. Davis outlines in his paper “Tibet and China's 'National Minority' Policies.”

During the Tang Dynasty (618-907), a loose imperial association existed between the two countries. (Davis 436)  
    
At the time of the Yuan Dynasty (1270-1368),  when the Mongol Empire controlled the countries, it administered Tibet and China separately. (Davis 437)

In the Ming Dynasty (1368-1644), a diplomatic relationship existed between China and Tibet with the latter acting as intermediaries between the Chinese Ming court and the Mongol emperors. (Davis 437)

During the Qing Dynasty (1644-1912), China invaded and occupied Tibet but the latter was treated as a vassal state and indirectly ruled as part of China's external empire. (Davis 436-7) There was no serious incorporation of Tibet and it remained separate from China. (Davis 437)  


2. b) How would you characterize the pre-1911 relationship between China and Xinjiang? Kerry Hall

                                   
Central Eurasian Studies professor Gardner Bovingdon examines the historical relationship of Xinjiang and China in his book chapter on Xinjiang. He concludes Xinjiang became a province of China after the latter forcibly colonized it.  
 
 During the Qing Dynasty (1644-1912), the Chinese Emperor Qianlong conquered Xinjiang in 1759 and ruled the area as a colony with military garrisons. (Bovingdon 338)
 
 In the late 1700s, the Chinese promoted immigration to the region such that, by the early 1800s, the half-million population was made up of about 165,000 Hans and 300,000 Turkis (Uyghurs). (Bovingdon 338)  
 
  In 1864, a warlord from Kokand (Uzbekistan) led an uprising with the Turkis (Uyghurs) and gained control over almost all of the area. (Bovingdon 338)  However, in the mid-1870s, the Chinese reclaimed most of the territory following a military campaign. (Bovingdon 338)  
  
  In 1884, the colony was designated a formal province named Xinjiang (New Frontier), although Han immigration decreased following the transformation. (Bovingdon 338)
